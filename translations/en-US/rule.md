# Composition of Mahjong Tiles #
1. This ruleset uses a set of 92 mahjong tiles, without flower tiles.
2. The 92 tiles consist of 23 kinds, 4 of each.
3. Tiles of the same kind have the same face and the same function; this is called a "kind". Tiles of different kinds must be clearly distinguishable from one another.
4. The 23 kinds split into "number tiles" and "honor tiles", or into "terminal or honor" tiles and "simple" tiles.
5. Number tiles: three suits, comprising the "Dot suit", the "Bamboo suit", and the "Character suit". Each suit has 6 kinds numbered 1 to 6, for 18 kinds in total.
6. Honor tiles: the three "dragons" -- White, Green, Red -- and the two "luminaries" -- Sun, Moon -- for 5 kinds in total.
7. Terminal-or-honor tiles: the 1 and 6 of each number suit (6 kinds) plus the 5 honor tiles, 11 kinds in total, are called terminal-or-honor tiles.
   * Terminal tiles: the part of the terminal-or-honor tiles excluding the honor tiles is called terminal tiles.
8. Simple tiles: the 2 through 5 of each number suit, 12 kinds, are simple tiles.

# Dealer and Seat Honor #
1. In a round, each of the three players holds a seat position, going counter-clockwise in the order White, Green, Red.
2. The dealer is the White player; the other two are collectively the "non-dealers".
3. Seat honor: the honor tile matching one's own seat position is the "seat honor"; the remaining dragon tiles are "guest honors".
4. Passing the deal: after each round, the former Green player becomes the dealer of the new round.
5. Cycle: every three rounds form a cycle.
6. Keeping the deal: if the winner of the round is the dealer, the deal does not pass; this is "keeping the deal". The next round is called the "first keep"; keeping the deal grants no bonus points.
7. A game may leave keeping the deal disabled; competitive rules must not enable it.

# Game Flow #

## Setup ##
Every game begins with a seating draw; every round begins with breaking the wall and shuffling.

### Position Roll ###
Rolling the dice means rolling two six-sided dice numbered 1 to 6.

Starting the count at 1 on the roller and going counter-clockwise, count up to the total rolled; the final position is the "position roll". That is: 4, 7 and 10 land on the roller; 2, 5, 8 and 11 on the next seat; 3, 6, 9 and 12 on the previous seat.

### Seating Draw ###
The procedure is as follows:
1. The three players first sit down in any order.
2. Take the three honor tiles White, Green, Red, shuffle them face down, and lay them in a single row with their long edges flush against each other.
3. Take any one odd-numbered tile and one even-numbered tile, place them face up at the two ends of the row, forming a row of five.
4. Any player rolls the dice and counts the position roll; the player thus indicated rolls again.
5. From the position roll of the second roll, each player in turn takes one honor tile going counter-clockwise. If the rolled total is odd, tiles are taken from the odd-tile end toward the even-tile end; if even, from the even-tile end toward the odd-tile end.
6. Whoever draws the "White" honor tile is the initial dealer and sits at the position marked "White" on the table, or need not move if there is none. Whoever draws "Green" sits to their right (the next seat), and whoever draws "Red" sits to their left (the previous seat).

## Cycle Honor ##
Every three rounds form a cycle; the cycle honor rotates through White, Green, Red.
By length, games split into the full match (three cycles: White, Green, Red) and the White-cycle match (only the White cycle).
The cycle honor serves only as a record-keeping aid; changing it requires no new seating draw and does not affect passing the deal, and every round still begins with the White player drawing and discarding.

## Round ##
A round begins with the shuffle and ends with a win or an exhaustive draw.

### Shuffle ###
For a hand shuffle, all mahjong tiles are placed face down and the three players thoroughly mix them by hand.
Each non-dealer then takes 30 tiles, stacking two tiles into one stack, and arranges the 15 stacks in a row long-edge to long-edge; the dealer takes 32 tiles, 16 stacks in total, called the wall.
Once built, push the rows to the center so the three walls form an approximate open-sided square or triangle, completing the shuffle.

### Breaking the Wall ###
1. The dealer rolls the dice and counts the position roll to determine which player is the "wall opener".
2. The wall opener rolls the dice and counts the position roll to determine in front of which player's wall the drawing begins.
3. Using the dealer's roll count, the wall opener pushes off that many stacks from the far right end of the wall toward the left, setting them aside as the "wall tail". The portion to the left of the divide is the "wall head".

### Deal ###
Tiles are taken clockwise, starting from the wall head.
1. The White player first takes two stacks, then the Red and Green players each take two stacks in that order.
2. Repeat the previous step three times, until each player has 8 tiles.
3. The three players in turn each take one stack from the wall head, then the White player takes one more tile from the wall head.
4. At this point the dealer should have 11 tiles and each non-dealer 10 tiles.
5. During the deal, before the tile count is confirmed correct, do not touch the underside of the tiles or look at your hand; keep the tiles face down until all three players confirm the deal counts are correct.

### Draw-and-Discard ###
1. After the deal, the White player discards one tile first.
2. In turn, each player draws one tile from the wall head and then discards one tile into the river. The river is arranged neatly five tiles per row; once a row of five is full, continue on a second row. Before the round ends, no tile in the river may be taken except by a "claim".
3. After someone makes a "call", once the claimer discards a tile, the draw-and-discard resumes from their next seat.
4. Draw-and-discard continues until someone wins or an exhaustive draw occurs.

### Call ###
1. A discarded tile may be "claimed" by another player to win by discard or to complete a "basic meld".
2. Only the tile just discarded may be claimed; once the next player discards, the previously discarded tile can no longer be claimed.
3. To claim in order to complete a meld, the whole meld must be revealed as an open meld, placed in an obvious spot distinct from the river and the concealed set.
4. There are four kinds of call: win, chow, pung, and kong; priority for simultaneous claims is covered in the detailed rules.
5. The caller must call first. A non-caller has no priority, and other players may ignore their action and continue draw-and-discard.

#### Chow ####
1. If a tile discarded by the previous seat combines with two tiles in one's concealed set to form a "sequence", one may call "chow" and take that tile together with one's two concealed tiles to form an "open sequence".
2. Besides being placed in the position for claimed melds, this meld is arranged low to high from left to right, with the tile discarded by the previous seat laid sideways to show it was taken by chow.
3. After calling "chow", wait one second to confirm no other player has another call, then reveal one's two concealed tiles to form the open sequence.

#### Pung ####
1. If a tile discarded by another player matches the kind of one's pair in concealed set, one may call "pung" and take that tile to form an "open triplet".
2. The punged tile must indicate which player it came from: if discarded by the next seat, lay the rightmost tile sideways; if by the previous seat, lay the leftmost tile sideways.

#### Kong ####
1. Four identical tiles become a "quartet" after a "kong". A kong is of three kinds: "concealed quartet", "added quartet", and "claimed quartet". A kong must be followed by a "replacement draw".
2. Concealed quartet: when the hand holds four identical tiles, one may make a concealed quartet. It is done after one's draw (including after the dealer's deal, or after a replacement draw) or after a chow or pung, before discarding. After calling "kong", first reveal the four tiles to the other players to confirm they are identical, then place them in the claim area with the middle two face up and the two ends face down.
3. Added quartet: when the hand holds a tile identical to one's open triplet, one may call kong to make an added quartet. Its timing is the same as the kong for concealed quartet. After calling "kong", reveal that tile and add it to the end of the open triplet not laid sideways, forming an "open quartet".
4. Claimed quartet: if a tile discarded by another player matches one's concealed triplet, one may call "kong" and take that tile with the concealed triplet to form an "claimed quartet". As with the open triplet, indicate which player it came from: if discarded by the next seat, lay the rightmost tile sideways; if by the previous seat, lay the leftmost tile sideways.
5. When an opponent's discard matches one's open triplet, no kong may be made.
6. Replacement draw: after a kong, a "replacement draw" must be made, drawing one tile from the "wall tail", called the "replacement tile". A replacement tile that exactly completes a win is called "After a Kong", a win by draw. After the replacement draw, if there is no win, one tile must be discarded.

#### Robbing a Kong ####
When another player makes an "added quartet" and that tile is exactly one of the tiles one is waiting on, one may call "win" or "wu" and take that tile to complete the win, called "Robbing a Kong"; the kong-maker is then treated as dealing in.

#### Win ####
1. Waiting: when the owned set is one tile short of completing a winning set, it is "waiting".
2. Win by draw: during draw-and-discard, when the drawn tile exactly completes the winning set, it is a "win by draw". The player should declare "win", "wu" or "zimo" to announce the win.
3. Win by discard: when a tile discarded by another player exactly completes the winning set, one may call "win" or "wu" and take that tile to complete the win. This is a "win by discard"; the act of discarding that tile is "dealing in".
4. On winning, reveal the concealed set and lay out the winning set, split into open-meld and concealed-meld areas, then score by this winning set; after the other players confirm the winning set and the calculated score are correct, the scores are paid (see "Winning Set", "Scoring" and "Payment").
5. As long as the winning set is valid, one may win regardless of whether there are points; there is no restriction other than fake waiting.

### Exhaustive Draw ###
When 12 tiles remain in the wall after draw-and-discard, these 12 tiles are called the "dead wall"; they may not be moved and must stay face down until the next round begins. If the tile then discarded (the last discard) is not won by anyone, an exhaustive draw is declared: all scores stay unchanged, and the deal passes to the next round.

## End ##
After the final round ends in a win or an exhaustive draw, the game ends.

# Winning Set #
1. A basic winning set consists of one pair and 3 melds, 11 tiles in total (where a quartet counted as 3 tiles); each meld may be a "sequence", "triplet" or "quartet" from the basic melds.
2. The owned set must fully match a winning set to win; claiming a win without matching counts as a "invalid win"; see the winning part of the penalties.
3. As long as a winning set is valid, one may still win with no scoring hand at all; it simply scores nothing and is as effective as any scoring win.

## Basic Melds ##
1. Sequence: three tiles of the same suit with consecutive numbers form a "sequence".
2. Triplet: three tiles of the same kind form a "triplet".
3. Quartet: four tiles of the same kind become a "quartet" after a "kong"; without a kong they do not count as a quartet.
4. Pair: two tiles of the same kind are a "pair".

## Open and Concealed Melds ##
If every tile of a meld was drawn, it is a concealed meld: the "concealed sequence", "concealed triplet", and "concealed quartet". A concealed quartet still requires a kong but cannot be robbed. If one of a meld's tiles was formed by a "claim" when another player put it into the river, it is an open meld: the "open sequence", "open triplet", "claimed quartet" and "added quartet".

## Scoring ##
To score, take the laid-out winning set and sum the scores of all hands the winning set satisfies; within the same category, only the highest-scoring qualifying hand counts. See the score table for hand scores.

# Detailed Rules and Penalties #

## Deal ##
1. Misroll: if there is an error during the dice roll, re-roll immediately once noticed. If the three players have already begun taking tiles, the roll already made stands.
2. Illegal wall break: if the wall is broken at the wrong place, correct it immediately. But if all three players have already taken three tiles, this break stands.
3. Illegal dealer: if someone wrongly acts as dealer and starts taking tiles from the wall, and it is caught before the correct White player takes tiles, reshuffle and re-deal. If caught before the correct White player's third draw, continue dealing until all three players have 8 tiles, then have the correct White player take the skip tile and proceed by the normal procedure. If it is noticed only after the correct White player has finished the third draw, that player loses the right to deal this round; the wrongful dealer serves as dealer for this round and continues it. After this round ends, the deal passes normally to the seat after the correct White player.
4. Wrong tiles taken: if someone takes the wrong tiles during the deal, correcting it before they look at their hand or touch the tile faces avoids penalty; otherwise those stacked tiles become live penalty tiles.
5. Exposing a wall tile: if someone accidentally flips a wall tile during the deal, and those are tiles they were to take, treat it as an exposed owned set; otherwise reshuffle and re-deal.
6. Deal dead set: if before the first discard into the river the deal count is found short, tiles may be taken from the wall head to make it up; if before looking at the owned set or touching tile faces the deal count is found excessive, a referee or the previous seat randomly removes the excess tiles and places them at the wall head to correct it. All other cases are treated as a long or short set.

## Discarding ##

### Details ###

1. Before the previous seat discards into the river, one may not reach to draw a tile or block others' view of the river. Drawing that does not follow normal procedure may be treated as a "long set".
2. When the discarder's next seat draws a tile from the wall head, they are deemed to have given up claiming the discarder's tile.
3. Discarding before drawing is treated as a "short set".
4. After the discarder discards, if the next seat has drawn and mixed the tile into their concealed set, other players lose the right to claim that tile in the river.
5. If after the discard the next seat has drawn but, before mixing it into the concealed set, someone claims, the next seat must place the tile beside the wall head as their tile for the next draw.
6. When discarding, one may not read out the name of the discarded tile. If this causes an opponent's illegal call, it is treated as a "long set".

### Penalties ###

1. Illegal draw: if a player draws the wrong tile or draws from the wall tail, the draw is treated as valid. If that tile has not been discarded, it becomes a live penalty tile; if the illegal draw happens to be a win by draw, it is treated as a long set.
2. Exposing wall tiles: if on draw one flip any tile at the wall head, one must draw that tile and it becomes a live penalty tile. If multiple tiles are flipped, it is treated as a long set.
3. Exposing an opponent's tiles: if one flips an opponent's owned set, the flipped player may designate, among the flipper's tiles, as many tiles as were exposed to become live penalty tiles.

## Claim ##

### Detailed Rules ###

1. When claiming simultaneously, priority from high to low is:
   1. A win by the discarder's next seat
   2. A win by the discarder's previous seat
   3. pung or kong
   4. chow
   When multiple players win simultaneously, take the single highest-priority player by the above order, called a "priority win". Aside from wins, if a chow caller has already revealed the open sequence after calling chow, other players lose the right to pung or kong that tile.
2. Only a legal claim has priority; against an incorrect claim, a lower-priority legal claim may cancel that incorrect claim.
3. A claim is judged by the call; if one does not call, players may ignore the action and continue draw-and-discard.
4. An open meld formed by a claim may not be moved again until the round ends, except to declare an added quartet.

### Penalties ###

1. Illegal call: if someone claims and reveals the meld but immediately finds the claim incorrect, upon immediate correction the illegally revealed hand tiles become live penalty tiles.
2. Changed call: if, after calling a claim but before revealing the meld, one changes one's mind to a different kind of claim, there is no penalty if it is a legal claim; an illegal claim is treated as a retracted call.
3. Retracted call: if someone calls chow, pung or kong for claiming discarded tile but changes their mind before revealing the meld, or has no correct meld to complete the claim, then upon cancelling the call they must reveal two tiles as penalty tiles.
4. Retracted kong: if one calls kong after one's draw but changes one's mind before revealing the meld, or has no correct meld to complete the kong, cancelling the kong is allowed.
5. Illegal meld: if, after claiming and revealing the meld, once anyone draws a tile from the wall or a discard is claimed, the open meld is found not to meet the sequence or triplet rules of the basic melds, one loses eligibility to win this round. If a quartet is found to be wrong after a replacement draw, besides losing eligibility to win, one may also no longer claim or make a kong.
6. Invalid win: if someone declares a win and reveals their whole hand, then the win is found incorrect, it is the same as a mis-call: the revealed hand tiles become penalty tiles and the round continues.
7. Retracted win: if someone declares a win but changes their mind before revealing the owned set, or finds they cannot correctly win, then upon cancelling the win, a referee designates two tiles to reveal as penalty tiles. If there is no referee, the other two players each designate one different tile to reveal. If the retracted-winner has two or fewer hand tiles, all must be revealed.
8. Before a win is confirmed valid, any owned set that the other two players reveal after calling win are deemed an illegal exposure; if the win is found to be a invalid win, the round continues and all illegally revealed tiles become penalty tiles.

## Penalty Tiles ##

1. If a player illegally reveals tiles from their hand, the exposed tiles must stay face up on the table, called a "live penalty tile".
2. A live penalty tile may be discarded on the first discard after it became a live penalty tile; thereafter it becomes a "dead penalty tile" and may no longer be discarded.
3. Penalty tiles are placed between the concealed set and the wall; if the wall nearest oneself has been drawn out, place them between the river and the concealed set, clearly separated from the river. Live penalty tiles are placed to the right of dead penalty tiles, clearly separated.
4. Penalty tiles may not be used to form open melds; a call may only form an open meld from tiles in the concealed set and the tile discarded into the river.
5. If a player attempts to claim with a penalty tile, it is handled as a illegal chow, illegal pung, illegal kong or invalid win by call type.
6. Penalty tiles restrict only open melds. A penalty tile may be used to make a concealed quartet, and may also win by draw.

## Dead Set ##

1. If someone is found to have a mismatched tile count in their owned set, they lose eligibility to win this round, called a "dead set".
2. Each player's concealed set and open melds, with a quartet counted as one meld of 3 tiles, total 10 tiles when it is not their turn to discard; before their own discard, after a claim or draw, they should have 11 tiles.
3. If owned set is short, it is a "short set": eligibility to win this round is lost and the player cannot win.
4. If owned set is excessive, it is a "long set": eligibility to win this round is lost and the player may make no call of any kind.
5. The above applies to dead sets caused by carelessness. Deliberate offenders should receive additional punishment.

## Win ##

1. A normal winning set must be laid out in the form of four groups: 2, 3, 3, 3. The single tile of a win by discard should be laid sideways, shown as an open meld. Open melds must indicate which player they were taken from; concealed melds must have no sideways tile, for easy identification.
2. Except for a false win, a retracted win, a win by discard on a penalty tile, and "fake waiting", any tiles satisfying the winning set may win.
3. If the winning set has no hand type, it is a "no-hand win", scored as zero points; otherwise it is the same as any ordinary scoring win.

### Fake Waiting ###

There are two kinds; a fake-waiting win is treated as a retracted win or an invalid win depending on whether the hand is exposed.
1. Fake waiting by self discard: the winner may not win by discard on any tile of the same kind as a tile they have discarded, called "fake waiting by self discard". Draw is not subject to this, nor are the other winnable tiles.
2. Fake waiting by same-cycle discard: after the winner discards a tile, until they next draw and discard is called "one cycle"; if a winnable tile appears and they do not call a win, within the same cycle they may not win by discard on the same kind of tile, though other winnable kinds may still win.
